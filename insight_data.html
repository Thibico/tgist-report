Insights - DATA

Civil servants are hesitant to share data that reflect challenges on the ground.

Incomplete or low-quality data may result from challenges on the ground such as inadequate staffing, low data literacy, or lack of technical support. This causes major delays in publishing data; in some cases, data is even fabricated to “look nice,” which can become a source of embarrassment for the department when data is verified. Moving forward, a key design question remains: how might we create space for the government to work with and be honest about incomplete data?

The colloquial term စာရင်းမငြိမ် (sa-yinn-ma-nyein) referring to incomplete or unfinalized data literally means “moving records.”

[Case Study] Housing the Township GIS Tool

  • Finding a home for the Township GIS Tool (TGIST) is an ongoing process beset by many uncertainties. While township offices would be responsible for making sure data is up-to-date, accurate, and shared to the right people, we still needed to find a place to securely house the physical servers – central repositories that store and ‘serve’ data to township offices over the internet.
  • The current arrangement is for TGIST to be handed over to respective state and regional governments. This runs counter to most e-government initiatives in Myanmar, which are almost exclusively housed in and managed by the Union-level bodies. We have, in addition, found that technical management capacity and infrastructure at the state and regional level might be insufficient for the needs of a tool like TGIST, though individual states and regions may differ.
  • A national data center being developed by the Ministry of Transport and Communication could well be the answer to our problem of housing TGIST. The Korea EXIM Bank-funded data center is to integrate all e-government technologies in line with the Myanmar e-Governance Master Plan (2016-2020).
  • A local, decentralized tool like TGIST is an alternative approach to e-government in Myanmar. Even though Union-level management makes pragmatic sense, top-down implementation could very well reinforce existing structures instead of forging novel pathways like we originally intended.
